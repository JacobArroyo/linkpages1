Childhood Years

I grew up as a dedicated swimmer.1 I spent most of my childhood training and competing from Grade 1 to Grade 10.2 I specialized in freestyle and backstroke, earning many medals while competing in the Philippine Swim League and numerous Speedo Events.3 During that time, I was also the team captain of the Mezza Swim Team, which strengthened my discipline and leadership with my teammates.4 Even though I wasn’t very social, I still took on the responsibilities given to me by my coach.5 I always had a world of my own, especially in school.6 I tended to be quiet and focused on my personal goals, finding comfort in my own space.7 Swimming taught me perseverance, resilience, and how to stay committed to long-term goals.8 I enjoyed pushing myself to improve, even on days when practice felt exhausting.9 My childhood in swimming shaped much of my identity, giving me confidence and a strong sense of discipline.10 I learned how to handle both wins and losses gracefully.11 I also developed a mindset of personal improvement that carried over into other aspects of my life.12 Even outside the pool, I often set goals for myself and worked quietly to achieve them, especially when reading books.13 Swimming was more than just a sport, it became a foundation for my character.14 It taught me the importance of routine, patience, and self-motivation.15 I also learned how to balance leadership with humility among my students and teammates.16 The friendships I made in the swim team enriched my childhood, even if I remained naturally reserved.17 These experiences shaped who I am today, being focused, determined, and quietly ambitious.18 I learned to manage pressure at a young age, especially during important competitions.19 These lessons helped me stay calm and focused even in challenging situations.20


Teenage Years

As I entered my teenage years, I continued searching for what I wanted to do in the future.1 In senior high school, I took the ABM strand, inspired by the idea of becoming a rich person like the guy in the movie “The Wolf of Wall Street”.2 However, I eventually realized that the accounting side of business wasn’t really for me.3 In Grades 11 and 12, I also tried fencing and competed, but I realized I wasn’t cut out for it.4 Even with the long hours of training, often half the day outside of school, I wasn’t progressing as much as I wanted.5 Fencing taught me patience, strategy, and the value of persistence, even if it wasn’t my ultimate path.6 I continued to spend most of my time focusing on my own goals, maintaining the quiet, reserved nature I had carried from childhood.7 This period was important for self-discovery, helping me understand my strengths, weaknesses, and true interests and passion.8 I learned that trying new things is valuable, even if they don’t become a permanent pursuit for my future.9 My teenage years were a mix of experimentation, reflection, and preparation for what I truly wanted.10 They helped me develop resilience and humility.11 I also realized that personal growth doesn’t always require social interaction.12 I continued honing my discipline, balancing academics, sports, and personal goals.13 This stage of life really helped me return to my original passion with clarity and purpose.14 I understood that it was okay to try and fail, as long as I learned from the experience along the way.15 I began to appreciate the importance of time management and prioritization in achieving my goals.16 I also recognized that persistence often matters more than raw talent.17 These years taught me to trust my instincts when choosing which paths to pursue.18 By the end of my teenage years, I felt more confident in my decisions and more prepared for the challenges ahead.19 I carried these lessons forward as I approached the next chapter of my life.20


College Years

When I reached college, I returned to what I had always been passionate about.1 I shifted to BSIT, choosing it over Computer Engineering and Computer Science mainly because I disliked Calculus.2 During this time, I discovered powerlifting through friends at the gym, and this sport immediately felt right for me.3 I trained, ate, slept, and dedicated myself fully to the sport, enjoying every moment.4 Eventually, I competed in August 2025, a huge milestone that pushed me even deeper into the sport.5 Today, I am not only a competitive powerlifter but also a coach, guiding my athletes as they prepare for their first competition in early February 2026.6 Powerlifting gave me direction, a way to turn my passion into a job, and a purpose to be the strongest in the 74kg division.7 It has also taught me leadership, mentorship, and the importance of motivating others.8 College became a space for growth, both academically and personally.9 I learned to balance studies, training, and coaching responsibilities.10 Powerlifting reinforced discipline, perseverance, and the value of consistent effort.11 I also developed a sense of fulfillment in helping others achieve their goals.12 This sport shaped my identity in ways similar to swimming, but with a deeper understanding of strength, both physical and mental.13 Today, I continue to pursue excellence in my sport while preparing for a future that combines my passion for technology and athletics.14 I have learned to embrace challenges as opportunities for growth.15 Time management has become crucial, as I juggle training, coaching, and academics.16 I also discovered the importance of setting both short-term and long-term goals.17 Through powerlifting, I have built resilience that extends beyond the gym.18 These experiences continue to shape my mindset, preparing me for whatever comes next.19 They have also taught me to stay adaptable, ready to face challenges both in and out of the gym.20
