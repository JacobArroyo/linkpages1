I Got My First Nintendo DSI

I got my first Nintendo DSI with a red shell and a Japanese firmware when I was 7 years old. It was the device that truly started my journey with digital technology and software such as the flipbook drawing app called FlipNote Studios and the photo and audio editor it had. I was immediately fascinated by the screen, buttons, and the idea that I could control what is happening in this very device.

As a young kid, I spent countless hours experimenting with my Nintendo DSI settings and memorizing every button's look and effect since the console and game cartridges were all Japanese. I mostly relied on trial and error which taught me to observe patterns rather than depend on the instructions which is kind of fun. Over time I became confident in using the device and navigating the functions and features it has. These experiences helped me shape my problem-solving skills and made me more interested in technology especially in gaming.

Using the Nintendo DSI also helped me realize and understand how technology has this response to user input. Therefore I learned that every action like pressing a button or selecting an option produced a specific result on the screen, especially that one time I realized that some same game development companies have the same button functions, navigations, and feel in the game or the menu. So then I began to think about how games and applications were designed. This experience really made me interested and curious about software, systems, and game development.


My Mother's Co-worker Gifted Me A Laptop

Back in the early 2010s, my mother's co-worker would always invite us on the weekends so that us kids could hang out while they take care of their business. I would usually spend that time with Tin-Tin playing computer games like Plants Vs. Zombies, a bowling simulator, and a bunch of 2000s Electronic Arts games. I instantly got hooked on these games quickly and one day Tin-Tin's older brother Cedric asked me if I wanted a laptop. At first, I thought he was joking, but he ended up showing me his Acer Aspire 4736Z laptop.

After getting the laptop, my mom took me to Sta. Mesa sometimes to choose some games, especially Hidden Object Adventure games from developers like Big Fish Games and Elephant Studios. These games were burned on CDs, so I had to load them on my laptop's CD player and run the installation wizard. It was an entirely new experience, learning how to install and manage software on a real computer for the first time because I'm very shy when it comes to computer shops.

Not to forget, I also got an original CD copy of Plants vs Zombies, which required the disc to run and read on the disc itself rather than installing it on the computer. Over time, the disc got full of scratches, and every time I wanted to play, it felt like a gamble whether it would even work. Despite these issues, having that laptop gave me my first real taste of PC gaming and computer management, and it completely fascinated me how a computer works which is why I always stick around on the Discovery channel waiting for the show How Do They Do It?


Fascinations with Breadboard and CPU

I have a long-time fascination with breadboards and electronic circuits, but for a while, I only relied on YouTube tutorials. Because of this, I found it very challenging especially to read circuit schematics, which are very important when building a more complex project. I would usually spend hours thinking about what to try next until one day I decided to attempt building a computer on a breadboard. I researched whether it was even possible even with only a transistor and a bunch of LEDs instead of integrated circuit logic gates, and while it was extremely difficult, I realized I could build it if I try to design every part myself with the help of other project schemas and online forums.

Currently, I have successfully built a clock module using a 555 timer with aluminum and ceramic capacitors and a bunch of resistors which control the clock speed and in the future I might as well change that function to using a potentiometer instead to easily change the speed. Onto the next part, I have 4 Set and Reset Latches (SR Latch) which stores 1 bit each, which makes the total input 4 bits. Currently, I am scouring the internet for schematics and studying how others built their own designs using only transistors. I am learning how to make logic gates, static memory cells, and how the clock module interacts with the register and how I would incorporate the data bus lane to the next part of the processor.

Despite struggling to read the schematics, I am determined to create my own design. Each experiment teaches me something new, and I am constantly improving my understanding of how a central processing unit and a group of transistors work. This project has not only improved my skills in electronics but also fuels my passion for computers and system design, showing me how much creativity and logic could go into making technology function.
